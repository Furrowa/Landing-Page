Overwatering vs Underwatering: How Oriva Prevents Both

May 1, 20265 min read

Two of the most common — and most costly — irrigation mistakes in Indian farming are opposite extremes: overwatering and underwatering. Both reduce yield. Both cost money. And both happen for the same reason: farmers have no reliable way to know how much water is actually in the soil right now.

The Real Cost of Overwatering

  • Root rot and fungal disease — waterlogged soil suffocates roots and promotes disease
  • Nutrient leaching — excess water pushes nutrients below the root zone
  • Wasted electricity — every unnecessary pump run adds to your electricity bill
  • Wasted water — in water-scarce areas, this directly impacts long-term farm viability

The Real Cost of Underwatering

  • Crop stress — wilting, leaf scorch, and stunted growth
  • Reduced yield — water stress at critical growth stages (flowering, grain fill) causes permanent yield loss
  • Poor quality produce — fruits and vegetables affected by water stress have lower market value

Why Fixed Schedules Cause Both Problems

Most farmers irrigate on a fixed calendar — every 2 days, or every Monday and Thursday. This approach ignores what actually determines crop water need:

  • How much it rained recently (and how much the soil actually absorbed)
  • Current temperature and wind (evapotranspiration rate)
  • Crop growth stage and current water demand
  • Soil type — sandy soils drain fast; clay holds water longer

A fixed schedule that works in April may overwater in May and underwater in June.
